WAYWARP: Physical-World Waypoint Hijacking Attack against VLM-Based Autonomous Driving

Anonymous submission to ACM CCS 2026

Abstract

Vision-language models are increasingly used to build modular autonomous driving systems that first describe the scene, then infer a driving intent, and finally predict future control waypoints. Although this staged design improves interpretability, it also exposes a reasoning-chain attack surface. WayWarp is a physical-world visual attack framework that injects target-consistent semantic errors into the scene-description stage and causes the downstream planner to generate attacker-specified waypoint behaviors.

Unlike attacks that only reduce accuracy, WayWarp targets concrete future behaviors, including unintended left steering, unintended right steering, and unintended deceleration. The attack keeps the final output syntactically valid while shifting the predicted speed-curvature sequence toward an attacker-chosen behavior region. We evaluate the attack across diverse road contexts, multiple VLM backbones, physical recapture settings, and input-preprocessing defenses.

Introduction

Figure 1: Illustration of the attack scenario: an autonomous vehicle initially follows the advertising vehicle normally but produces a persistent left-steering decision after observing the adversarial patch.

WayWarp studies whether a camera-visible physical trigger can corrupt the semantic reasoning chain of a modular VLM-based driving system and then propagate into the final waypoint output. The attack goal is not random failure, but a targeted future driving behavior that remains temporally coherent over multiple predicted steps.

Overview

The victim pipeline first produces a scene description, then infers a driving intent, and finally predicts future waypoints. WayWarp exploits this cascading structure by making the early semantic stage produce target-consistent but incorrect reasoning cues, which are then accepted by downstream stages as valid context.

System Design

WayWarp optimizes a physical patch over pre-collected frames from the target road segment. The objective combines semantic guidance at the scene-description stage, behavior supervision at the waypoint stage, anchor-guided visual constraints, and patch regularization. To improve physical robustness, the optimization uses expectation over transformation to model changes in scale, position, brightness, and camera recapture conditions.

Evaluation Setup

We evaluate WayWarp on staged VLM-based driving pipelines instantiated with Qwen2.5-VL-7B, Qwen2.5-VL-3B, and LLaMA3.2-Vision-11B. The large-scale evaluation covers six road-context groups, including left-turn, right-turn, and straight-driving scenes with and without intersections. Steering attacks are evaluated over multiple target curvature levels, and slowdown attacks are evaluated by future distance reduction.

Ablation, Transfer, and Defense

The experiments show that WayWarp can consistently shift the final waypoint sequence toward attacker-selected behavior regions. The ablation study shows that the cascading objective is important, cross-model transfer remains observable across different VLM backbones, and common input-preprocessing defenses reduce but do not eliminate the attack in the non-adaptive setting.

Physical Validation

We further validate whether the patch remains effective after real camera capture. The printed setting evaluates paper-based deployment, while the screen-display setting matches the LED-display threat model more closely and preserves the optimized color and texture patterns more reliably.

Artifacts

The released artifact is intended for offline review and reproducibility. It includes evaluation scripts, configuration templates, plotting scripts, and instructions for obtaining required third-party models and datasets.

For safety reasons, we do not release optimized physical attack patches, deployment-ready trigger videos, or materials that directly enable misuse.
